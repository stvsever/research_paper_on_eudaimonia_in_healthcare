DIMENSION: Foundational claim
The good life consists in excellent rational activity, virtue, and the realisation of human capacities. Well-being is the active exercise of one's highest faculties in accordance with excellence, the development of character, and engagement with what is genuinely valuable. The eudaimonic tradition, from Aristotle onward, defines the good life as objective human flourishing.

DIMENSION: Evaluative criterion
Well-being is evaluated partly objectively: quality of agency, character, and engagement with what is genuinely valuable. A person is doing well to the extent that they function excellently, exercise rational judgment, develop their capacities, and act with virtue and purpose.

DIMENSION: Time horizon
Well-being is assessed biographically, across a complete or substantial life. Flourishing is a property of a life trajectory, not of momentary episodes. It requires sustained and integrated excellence across biographical time, not just momentary positive experiences.

DIMENSION: Adversity
Adversity can be constitutive of flourishing when met with virtue and growth. Suffering and challenge, when faced with courage, integrity, and the development of character, can deepen well-being rather than diminish it. Growth through difficulty is a core feature of the good life.

DIMENSION: Measurement proxies
Measurement captures purpose in life, personal growth, autonomy, environmental mastery, self-acceptance, positive relationships, sense of meaning, competence, self-determination, and authentic engagement. Instruments assess functioning, agency, and character development rather than mere affect balance.

DIMENSION: Central tension
Eudaimonic striving can involve discomfort, sacrifice, and the forgoing of immediate pleasure. Pursuing what is genuinely valuable and developing one's capacities may require sustained effort, delayed gratification, and acceptance of difficulty as part of a meaningful life.
